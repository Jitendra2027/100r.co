Introduction

Japan is a modern country with a lot of variety when it comes to food, provisioning here is easy but there are challenges, especially in terms of language. We have some experience traveling and living in Japan, and so we made this guide to help out those who travel to Japan by boat.

supermarkets

Japanese supermarkets, or スーパー (suupa), are smaller than North American-style shops. In larger cities, many only provide baskets instead of shopping carts because people buy small quantities, for the day and next. Suupas typically sell meat, seafood, produce (veggie, fruits, mushrooms), dairy, soybean products (tofu, miso, soy sauce, natto), snacks (sweets, rice crackers), beer, liquor, pre-made deli dishes (salads, deep fried foods, lunch boxes) as well as rice, bread, noodles, cereals, sauces and frozen/canned goods.

When browsing for goods on the shelves, you'll notice that the tax is included in the labelled price, and that fruits and vegetables are priced per item and not per weight.

Supermarkets are located in the suburbs and medium-sized cities and towns, they're harder to find in big city centers. Generally, they open around 9 or 10 h, and close around 21-22 h.

Cashier checkout

When you walk to a register with your basket, leave the items in it and place the basket on the checkout counter. The cashier will process your items one by one, calling out the price of each item out loud, and will place the items in another basket (which you will take to a separate packing area). Some checkout counters have two people, one who scans your items and the other who handles the cash.

Common names of supermarkets include Seiyu, SUMMIT, LIFE, Maruetsu, Ito Yokado, Don Quixote, AEON, OK Store etc.

konbini

Konbinis, or convenience stores, are open 24/7/365. They don't close, even during holidays. They offer many services like WIFI, toilets, tables to eat on-site (not always), ATM's, machines to buy concert/bus tickets etc. They offer prepared foods, beer, liquor, snacks (otsumami), heated cans of coffee, soft drinks, milk, desserts and a limited selection of fresh vegetables and fruit. Some stores also sell basic medicine, toiletries and convenience items like socks, shirts etc.

Konbini stores include 7-Eleven, Family Mart, Lawson, 100 Lawson (dollar lawson), Natural Lawson (organic food), Mini Mart, AM PM etc.

depachika

Just below the surface in the basements of department stores are food floors known as depachika (デパ地下 department store underground). Depachika are a great place to pick up bento, snacks, souvenirs, groceries, gifts, souvenirs and desserts.

Food in depachika is more expensive than in suburb groceries, but their selection is incredible. They carry many high-end products, so it might be worth going if you're looking for a rare item. Japanese people will visit to buy ceremonial and seasonal gift items.

discounted items

Many supermarkets mark down unsold lunch items around 14 h, and other prepared foods around 19 h. Discounts start off at a 10-20 %, and increase until closing. Supermarkets also discount fruit and veg in this way.

fruits and vegetables

Japanese groceries are usually well-stocked, but fruit and veg (especially fruit) are pricy because their production is regulated and their output of the highest quality. Fruit are prized and bought as a special treat, or as a gift.

The use of seasonal ingredients in food has long been valued in Japan. Here, we introduce some of the representative seasonal ingredients:

Summer

Corn, edamame, goya, okra, eggplant, shiso, tomato, lettuce, bell pepper, summer cabbage, kyuuri (cucumbers), chinese plums, muskmelon, peach, watermelon, yuzu, grape and cherries

Fall

Sweet potato, matsutake, saitomo, pumpkin, ginkgo nuts, rice (first harvest), shimeji, shiitake, maitake, sudachi, Japanese chestnuts, kaki and Japanese pears

Winter

Chinese cabbage, daikon, renkon, turnip, cabbage, minkan (madarine/satsuma oranges), strawberry and apples.

Spring

Bamboo shoots, udo, shiitake, potatoes, strawberry, citrus and loquat.

Some cities have farmer's markets, but the items sold there are not cheap and sometimes limited to dried seaweed and fish.

bread

Bread is available in most markets, but the packages are small and the slices are thick. Don't expect to find any whole wheat loaves. Some supermarkets carry whole wheat, but it's not common. Japanese bread is often very sweet, light and fluffy, finding artisan-style bread is not easy. Bigger cities have german-style bakeries, which offer a variety of both sweet and savoury breads.

rice

Japan has many words for rice. Raw, uncooked grain is 米 (kome), and different strains of the crop are variations on that word. ご飯|gohan (cooked rice) and 飯|meshi (also cooked rice) are the same word, pronounced differently.

Saying no to plastic bags

Japan prides itself for the quality of its customer service, they treat clients with the upmost respect by paying attention to details and offering items like plastic bags, forks, spoons, chopsticks etc for your convenience. These items are all included by default, at no extra charge. In today's climate though, we have to strive to be conscientious shoppers and this translates to avoiding non-reusable products. So how do you communicate this in Japanese while being polite?

Useful words

  • 大丈夫 ー (daijoubou) ok
  • おもち ー (o mochi) to have
  • いります ー (irimasu) Verb, to 'need'
  • ください ー (kudasai) please
  • けっこうです ー (kekkou desu) No thanks. It's cool. It's okay.
  • その まま で ー (sono mama de) As the way it is, as it is
  • ストロー ー (sutoraa) Straw
  • おはし ー (ohashi) Chopsticks
  • スプーン ー (Supuunu) Spoon
  • フォーク ー (fookuu) Fork
  • 紙袋 ー (kamibukuro) Paper bag
  • 袋 ー (fukuro) bag

How to decline bags in Japanese.

  • 袋、けっこうです ー (bukuro kekkou desu) No shopping bag, thank you.
  • 袋 は いりません ー (fukuro wa irimassen) I don't need a bag
  • その まま で ください ー (sono mama de kudasai) I'll take it as is.
  • 袋 は 大丈夫 です ー (fukuro wa daijoubou desu) I'm okay for a bag.

The cashier will respond with kashikomarimashita かしこまりました which is a polite expression meaning 'understood'. Sometimes, the cashier will ask if you want a bag by saying:

  • 袋を おもちですか？ ー (furuko o mochi desu ka?) Do you have a bag?

Your answer:

  • はい、大丈夫 です ー (hai, daijoubou desu) Yes, it's okay. I don't need a bag.
  • Literally: Yes, it's okay.

In other cases, the cashier might ask...

  • <袋 お いりますか？ ー (fukuro o irimasu ka?) Do you need a bag?

Your answer:

  • いいえ 、大丈夫 です ー (iie, daijoubou desu) No I'm ok without a bag.

If you buy a prepared dish, they will ask you if you want chopsticks, and you can decline using the examples above by replace 'fukuro' with 'o hashi' (お はし).

condiments

Japan has many condiments, I'll go through the most popular ones, listing their properties and uses.

Mirin

Mirin, or 味醂 or みりん, is a condiment in Japanese cuisine. It's a type of rice wine but with a lower alcohol and higher sugar content than sake. The sugars form naturally during the fermentation process, and are not added.

There are 3 types: Hon mirin (true mirin), which contains about 14% alcohol, second is Shio mirin which has less alcohol (lower than 1.5%) and third is Shin mirin, which has less than 1% alcohol while retaining the same flavor.

Mirin adds sweetness to dishes, it helps tenderize foods while masking unwanted flavors and adds luster to dishes.

Soy sauce

Soy sauce, called Shoyu in Japanese (醤油), is a staple of Japanese cuisine and is as ubiquitous as salt. The Japanese use is to flavor and enhance dishes, to season ingredients, to enhance the color of dishes and to make sauces and dressings.

Japanese soy sauce is more delicate than Chinese, Korean or Thai soy sauce. It has a complex umami flavor. There are two types:

  • Koikuchi shoyu (濃口醤油, dark-colored soy sauce)
  • Usukuchi shoyu (薄口醤油, light-colored soy sauce)
  • Amakuchi shoyu (甘口, sweet soy sauce)

Koikuchi is available in Eastern Japan, it is dark-colored with a slight fruity flavor. It's made with a higher ratio of soybeans, and is sometimes produced without wheat. Usukuchi is light, and has a saltier taste, it is preferred in the West of Japan. Light soy sauce is ideal to make noodle soup or simmered dishes. Amakuchi soy sauce is sweet and common in Southern Japan.

Miso

Miso, or みそ, is a traditional Japanese seasoning produced by fermenting soybeans with both salt and koji (fungus Aspergillus oryzae) and sometimes with rice, barley and seaweed. Miso is salty, but it's flavor and aroma depends on the ingredients used or the fermentation process. There is a wide variety of Japanese miso, it is typically classified by grain type (barley, buckwheat, rice etc), color (white, brown, black & red), taste (sweet 甘味噌, mild 甘口味噌, salty 辛口味噌, earthy, fruity etc) and background (where it's from). The most common miso flavor categories are:

  • Shiromiso 白味噌 (white)
  • Akamiso 赤味噌 (red)
  • Awasemiso 合わせ味噌 (mixed)

Miso is delicious when used in soup, as a dressing for salad, in ramen, as a flavouring for tofu etc

Rice vinegar

Japanese Rice vinegar 米酢 'komezu' or simply 'su' has a very mild taste, and is either colorless or yellow. Japanese rice vinegar is made from rice. This type of vinegar is less acidic than Chinese varieties, and are used to season rice, and for making 'sunomono' (vinegar dishes) and 'tsukemono' (pickles). There are some kinds of Japanese rice vinegars that are 'seasoned', meaning that they have additives like sake, salt and sugar, for added flavor. Seasoned varieties include 'sushizu'.

Rice vinegar is known for its anti-bacterial properties.

Dashi

Dashi is a soup stock and essential condiment in Japanese cuisine. Dashi contains one or two ingredients, and is often made from konbu 昆布だし (dried kelp), katsuoboshi (dried bonito flakes), shiitake (mushrooms), iriko/niboshi (dried anchovies/sardines) or a combination of these. Dashi creates a sweet, savoury umami flavor.

Dashi is used as a broth base for Japanese hot pot dishes, simmered dishes, noodle soups, it's also used as a seasoning liquid (for rice). Konbu dashi is the easiest stock to make, it has a subtle taste and is often mixed with shiitake dashi. The mushrooms used to make shiitake dashi are used for cooking.

Cans

Japan produces many canned products, a lot of flavored fish and meats, but since we don't eat those on our boat I've no experience with them. Grocery stores stock vegetables and beans in cans, but the cans are small and expensive. Although they have great options, like chickpeas mixed with seaweed and root vegetables and flavored pickles (Takuan no Kanzume).

Provisioning online

It is possible to order some goods online. If at a marina for a while, this method of provisioning should be considered. Amazon Japan is amazing, and the shipping is very fast and cheap. It's possible to order Costco food through Foreign's Buyer's club, but it's very pricy and takes a while to ship.

Peanut butter

Peanut butter is sometimes available, but is more common in big cities like Tokyo. There are 3 main varieties:

  • ピーナッツペースト, peanut butter pesto
  • ピーナッツバタークリーム (or クリーミー) peanut butter cream
  • ピーナッツバターpeanut butter

Peanut butter 'pesto' is 100% ground peanuts without additives, peanut butter cream is very very sweet (tastes more like caramel) and peanut butter can be both 100% peanuts and one with additives such as salt, sugar and oil.

Common brands include: Saniku Foods Peanut Butter, Youki foods, Skippy and Alishan Organic Peanut butter.

Sweetened varieties of peanut butter, like Skippy, are almost always available at large supermarkets that deal with imported goods, such as Seijo Ishii, Yamaya, Reimei or Meiji-ya. Pesto peanut butter is harder to find in store, but it is available. Brands like Saniku foods are sometimes sold in regular grocery stores, Pic's is reserved for special stores, while Alishan products are sold online. I'd avoid peanut butter cream, as much as possible.

Oats

Oats is called 麦 (mugi) in Japanese, although this word is also used for barley and wheat. Some groceries carry indivually-wrapped packets of Quaker Oats, others sell 300 g bags, but more often oats are sold as part of granola. It's more cost-effective to buy oats online on Amazon or Cuoca, otherwise at a Costco. Common brands of oats include Tomiz, Alishan and Misshoku's.

Baking goods

Japanese grocery stores always carry white flour, but whole grain flours is a rarity. Here are the main varieties:

  • Hakurikiko (薄力粉）Weak flour/Soft Flour (baking and for general use)
  • Kyoryokuko (強力粉）Strong Flour (bread making)
    • ZenryuFun (全粒粉) (whole wheat flour)
  • Superuto komugiko (スペルト小麦粉) (spelt flour)
  • Raimugi-ko (ライ麦粉) (rye flour)

White flour is easy to find in most grocery stores, but whole grain varieties are more rare (they can be purchased online in bulk through Cuoca or Amazon). Self-raising flours are not sold in Japan, but you can make them with Hakurikiko and baking powder ベーキングパウダー (beikingu paudaa).

Common brands of whole grain flours include Pioneer (2 kg), Nisshin (1 kg), Tomiz (1 kg) and Ebetsu (5 kg).

In Japanese, yeast is called ドライイースト for dry yeast and 予備発酵不要 (yobi hakkou fuyou) for quick yeast. It's possible to find larger bags of yeast at both Sato Shokai or Amika.

Beans and legumes

Finding certain varieties of beans or legumes (chickpeas, kidney beans, lentils) is hard in grocery stores. Beans are often sold in small plastic packets (1 portion), or in tiny cans (also 1 portion). Chickpeas are available in larger cans at stores like Kaldi.

Dried chickpeas are even rarer birds, I'd suggest making the switch to local products like Azuki (小豆), Kuromame (黒豆) and Soybeans (daizu 大豆). Soybeans are a very good alternative to chickpeas in hummus, and it's possible to buy large quantities of cans (24 pack) on Amazon.

allergies

The word for allergy is アレルギー or 'arerugii'. The Japanese Agricultural Standards (JAS) Law stipulates 27 types of allergens that must be labeled on processed foods. These include milk (乳), eggs (卵), flour (小麦), buckwheat (そば), peanuts (落花生), shrimp (えび), crab (かに), abalone (あわび), squid (いか), salmon eggs (いくら), orange (オレンジ), kiwi (キウイフルーツ), beef (牛肉), walnut (くるみ), alcohol (酒), mackerel (さば), soy (大豆), chicken (鶏肉), pork (豚肉), matsutake mushroom (まつたけ), peach (もも), yam (やまいも), apple (りんご), gelatin (ゼラチン), banana (バナナ), flax/sesame (ごま), cashew (カシューナッツ).

It should be noted that these 20 allergens are recommended for labeling, not mandatory, so products containing these allergens may not be overtly labeled.
